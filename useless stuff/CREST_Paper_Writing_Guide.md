# CREST Gold Award — Section-by-Section Paper Writing Guide

> **How to use this guide:** Write each section in the order given below. For every paragraph, the guide tells you exactly what to say, gives you a suggested opening sentence, and notes which CREST criterion (in square brackets) that paragraph provides evidence for. Never write more than one paragraph per instruction block — keep each paragraph focused.

**Target total:** 6,000–7,500 words (body text only, excluding references and appendices)
**Target pages:** ~20 pages, 12pt font, 1.5 line spacing, 2.5 cm margins
**Target figures:** 10 figures (all generated by the Colab notebook)

---

## Abstract (~180 words)

Write this **last**, after every other section is finished.

The abstract has exactly 5 sentences in this order:

1. **The problem.** One sentence stating why crop disease matters to Indian agriculture. Example: *"Crop disease causes an estimated 20–40% yield loss annually in India, disproportionately affecting smallholder farmers who lack access to timely expert diagnosis."*
2. **The gap.** One sentence identifying what existing research has not done. Example: *"Although numerous studies have applied transfer learning to plant disease classification, no published work compares multiple CNN architectures restricted to crops economically significant to Indian smallholders while simultaneously evaluating deployment feasibility on entry-level Android hardware."*
3. **What you did.** One sentence describing your method. Example: *"This study trained and evaluated three CNN architectures — MobileNetV2, ResNet50, and EfficientNetB0 — on a subset of the PlantVillage dataset filtered to five Indian staple crops: rice, wheat, tomato, potato, and maize."*
4. **Key result.** One sentence with your headline finding (fill in after running experiments). Example: *"[Best architecture] achieved [X]% test accuracy with a model size of [Y] MB and mean inference latency of [Z] ms on CPU."*
5. **Implication.** One sentence on what this means for farmers. Example: *"These results indicate that [best architecture] is deployable on smartphones costing under ₹8,000, making automated disease detection accessible to resource-constrained Indian farmers."*

**Criteria evidenced:** 4.5 (clear writing)

## Section 2 — Literature Review (~1,500 words)

> **Key rule for Gold:** Do not just describe papers one by one. Each paragraph must **synthesise** — find what papers agree on, where they disagree, and what is missing. The assessor is specifically looking for critical evaluation, not summaries.

### Paragraph 1: CNNs for Image Classification (context)
Introduce CNNs briefly. Mention LeNet → AlexNet → VGG → ResNet progression. End with: *"The introduction of residual connections by He et al. (2016) addressed the vanishing gradient problem that had limited the practical depth of earlier networks, enabling reliable training of networks exceeding 100 layers."*

**Cite:** He et al. 2016 (ResNet), Lecun et al. 1998 (LeNet)
**Criteria evidenced:** 4.1

---

### Paragraph 2: MobileNetV2 — Efficiency-Focused Architecture
Explain depthwise separable convolutions in one sentence. State that MobileNetV2 (Sandler et al. 2018) achieves near-ResNet accuracy at 14× fewer parameters. Note it is specifically designed for mobile deployment. **Critical note:** add a limitation: *"However, depthwise separable convolutions reduce representational capacity compared to standard convolutions, which may disadvantage classification of subtle disease symptoms sharing similar colour profiles."*

**Cite:** Sandler et al. 2018
**Criteria evidenced:** 4.1, 2.2 (critical evaluation)

---

### Paragraph 3: EfficientNetB0 — Compound Scaling
Explain that Tan & Le (2019) proposed scaling depth, width, and resolution simultaneously using a compound coefficient. State EfficientNetB0 is the smallest EfficientNet variant and achieves strong ImageNet accuracy at low parameter count. **Critical note:** *"Despite strong benchmark performance, EfficientNetB0's batch normalisation layers are sensitive to fine-tuning learning rate; Tan & Le report instability at learning rates above 1e-5, an issue replicated in this study."*

**Cite:** Tan & Le 2019
**Criteria evidenced:** 4.1, 2.2, 4.4 (foreshadows documented challenge)

---

### Paragraph 4: PlantVillage and Disease Classification
Describe the PlantVillage dataset (Mohanty et al. 2016): 54,000+ images, 38 disease categories, 14 crop species, controlled lab conditions. **Critical evaluation:** *"The dataset's primary limitation is that images were captured under controlled laboratory lighting, whereas Indian farmers photograph crops in the field under variable illumination. Accuracy figures reported on PlantVillage may therefore overestimate real-world performance — a limitation several authors have noted (Ramcharan et al. 2017; Barbedo 2019) but few have formally quantified."*

**Cite:** Mohanty et al. 2016; Ramcharan et al. 2017 (field conditions paper); Barbedo 2019
**Criteria evidenced:** 2.2, 4.1

---

### Paragraph 5: Transfer Learning for Agricultural Tasks
Synthesise what the literature agrees on: (a) fine-tuning pre-trained ImageNet weights outperforms training from scratch on small agricultural datasets; (b) two-phase training (freeze then unfreeze) is the most common and effective strategy; (c) data augmentation is universally applied. **Critical note:** *"Despite agreement on these principles, the literature is inconsistent in its choice of architectures — most studies test a single network and optimise it, making comparative conclusions across studies unreliable due to differing datasets and preprocessing pipelines."*

**Cite:** Yosinski et al. 2014; Ferentinos 2018; Chen et al. 2020
**Criteria evidenced:** 2.2, 4.1

## Section 3 — Methodology (~1,400 words)

### Subsection 3.1: Approaches Considered (~200 words)
Write three short paragraphs, each rejecting one alternative approach:

1. **Training from scratch:** Would require 100,000+ images per class to match transfer learning performance; PlantVillage is insufficient. Rejected.
2. **Classical machine learning (SVM, Random Forest):** Cannot process raw image pixels effectively without manual feature engineering; this was the approach taken in my Mango Project's sklearn classifier, which required manually computed features and cannot detect novel disease patterns. Rejected in favour of end-to-end learned representations.
3. **Single-architecture study:** Would answer a narrower question about whether CNNs work, not which CNN to choose for deployment. Rejected in favour of comparative evaluation.

**Criteria evidenced:** 1.3

*"Table 1 in the Results section reports the final class distribution after filtering. Class imbalance was addressed through class-weighted cross-entropy loss during training (see Section 3.4)."*

**Criteria evidenced:** 2.1, 4.2 (dataset licence)

### Subsection 3.3: Preprocessing and Data Split (~200 words)
State exactly: images resized to 224×224; pixel values normalised to [0,1]; dataset split 80% train / 10% validation / 10% test using stratified sampling to preserve class proportions. Show Figure 2 (sample images) here.

State augmentation applied to training set only: random horizontal flip, rotation ±15°, zoom 10%. Justify: *"Augmentation was restricted to the training set to prevent data leakage into validation and test evaluation."* Justify 80/10/10: *"A 10% test set (approximately 500–700 images) provides sufficient statistical power to estimate accuracy within ±3 percentage points at 95% confidence for the class sizes in this study."*

**Criteria evidenced:** 1.4, 4.2

### Subsection 3.4: Architecture and Training (~400 words)
Write one paragraph per architecture (MobileNetV2, ResNet50, EfficientNetB0), each covering: parameter count, ImageNet top-1 accuracy, the custom head added (GlobalAveragePooling2D → Dense(256, ReLU) → Dropout(0.5) → Dense(N, softmax)), and why the custom head is the same across all three (to ensure fair comparison).

Then write the training procedure:
- **Phase 1 (frozen):** Base model frozen, only custom head trained. Adam optimiser, learning rate 1e-3, 5 epochs. Rationale: *"Freezing the base in Phase 1 prevents catastrophic forgetting of ImageNet features while the randomly initialised head converges to a reasonable starting point."*
- **Phase 2 (fine-tuning):** Entire model unfrozen. Adam, learning rate 1e-5 (EfficientNetB0: 5e-6). 15 epochs. EarlyStopping patience=5. ModelCheckpoint saves best validation accuracy to Google Drive.
- **Loss function:** Categorical cross-entropy with class weights (computed via `sklearn.utils.class_weight.compute_class_weight`).

**Criteria evidenced:** 1.4, 4.1, 4.2

### Subsection 5.4: Methodological Influence on Results (~200 words)
Write three explicit method ↔ result connections — this is exactly what Criterion 3.2 requires:

### Subsection 5.5: Challenges and How They Were Solved (~200 words)
Document exactly four challenges (these are pre-planned as Criterion 4.4 evidence):

1. **Colab session timeout:** Colab's 12-hour limit would interrupt multi-model training. Solved by saving model checkpoints every 5 epochs to Google Drive and adding a resume-from-checkpoint cell.
2. **Class imbalance:** Wheat and rice had fewer samples than tomato. Solved by computing class weights via `sklearn.utils.class_weight.compute_class_weight` and passing to `model.fit()`.
3. **EfficientNetB0 fine-tuning degradation:** Initial fine-tuning at 1e-5 caused validation loss to spike in epoch 7. Solved by reducing fine-tuning learning rate to 5e-6, as recommended by Tan & Le (2019).
4. **tf-keras-vis version conflict:** `GradcamPlusPlus` raised `AttributeError` with TF 2.15 due to a breaking API change. Solved by adding a `GradientTape`-based fallback implementation.

**Criteria evidenced:** 4.4
